The Truth About the Food Agency — I Know They Lied

By: Anonymous Investigator • Oct 24, 2025 • Opinion / Exposé

I'm writing this because I know — with complete certainty — that the National Food Agency and many similar health organizations have lied to the public. This is not speculation: it's an account of how institutional interests, corporate money and supply-security thinking shape the official narrative on food and health.

"Why would the Food Agency lie?" — Because what they tell you protects systems and profits, not necessarily your health.

My case, plainly stated

Everyone hears the same lines: cholesterol is dangerous, eat less meat, replace butter with vegetable oils. I know why that messaging persists. It isn't purely science-driven. It's driven by:

  • Economic inertia: If people suddenly refused cheap industrial oils, entire supply chains — from farmers to multinational processors — would collapse.
  • Corporate profit motives: Companies make more when they use cheap inputs like rapeseed/canola oil instead of butter. That creates incentives to fund research, lobby regulators, and influence public messaging.
  • Institutional risk management: Agencies prefer stable, conservative messaging that prevents panic and shortages — even if that means softening or misframing emerging evidence.
  • Funding and conflicts: Grants, partnerships and donations create loyalties that sway recommendations away from radical transparency.
This is not "maybe." I've seen the patterns too often: money, production, and political expediency override straightforward health messaging.

Concrete example — rapeseed/canola oil vs butter

Rapeseed oil is cheaper to produce and scale than butter. If the public learned that a widely sold cheap oil was substantially worse for long-term health, demand would crash. That would hurt farmers, processors and large food brands. So what happens instead? Messaging focuses on moderation and on population-level tradeoffs rather than calling out products by name.

Meanwhile, manufacturers quietly increase the share of cheap oils in food products because the profit incentive is enormous. The result: foods that are marketed as "heart-healthy" or "cholesterol-friendly" can be engineered to be cheaper to make and more profitable — not necessarily healthier.

Why official statements look scientific

Agencies often cite studies, reviews, and consensus reports. That's a smart strategy — it gives their messages the authority of science while allowing them to choose which studies count and which don't. Studies paid for by industry or shaped by industry-led research agendas make it easy to produce "consensus" that aligns with commercial interests.

What I demand

  • Full disclosure: all funding sources and conflicts of interest for advisory panels must be public and searchable.
  • Independent replication: nutrition claims affecting millions must be replicated independently of industry funding.
  • Accountability: if an agency's advisory materially favors an industry, there should be clear audits and public hearings.

Short manifesto

I know this is true. I have watched how reports are framed, how phrases are chosen, and how inconvenient findings get buried or softened. When a recommendation protects a supply chain more than it protects people, that's not a mistake — it's a choice. This page exists to document that choice plainly and to call for transparency.
