The Big Food, Money Monopoly

Independent report by the Research Integrity Group (RIG) • Published: Aug 12, 2025 • Investigation team: policy analysts, nutrition scientists, investigative journalists
Executive Summary

In brief: Our multi-disciplinary investigative team has reviewed internal documents, procurement records, advisory panel minutes, and interviews related to the fictional public agency livsmedelsvärket. We find consistent patterns showing that (1) institutional priorities around food supply stability and cost containment repeatedly shaped public dietary messaging; (2) commercial partnerships and procurement incentives biased research agendas and guideline framing; and (3) the net effect was public guidance that prioritized systemic stability and industry interests over a stricter interpretation of emerging health evidence.

Key takeaway: The interaction of procurement economics, advisory composition and selective evidence framing produced a durable guidance regime that advantaged certain industry actors and dampened signals that could have materially changed population-level consumption patterns.

1. About this report

This exposé is the product of a six-month independent inquiry by RIG. We do not allege illegal conduct; rather we document how routine institutional choices — funding sources, procurement dependencies, advisory appointments, and evidence-selection rules — can systematically skew public-facing guidance.

2. Methodology

Our approach combined documentary analysis, data triangulation and interviews to reconstruct decision-making processes within livsmedelsvärket:

  • Document review: 43 internal policy memos, 12 procurement contracts, and minutes from 18 advisory committee meetings (redacted excerpts in Appendix A).
  • Interviews: semi-structured interviews with 14 current and former agency staff, 6 independent nutrition researchers, and 4 industry representatives.
  • Data analysis: procurement spending patterns (2015–2024), plus time-series of public guidance statements and media messaging to identify correlations between procurement changes and messaging shifts.

Note: All documentary evidence cited in this report is presented as redacted excerpts and summaries. Where primary documents cannot be published for legal or privacy reasons, we provide corroborating metadata and witness accounts.

3. Key findings

  1. Supply & procurement shape advisory framing. Procurement contracts show multi-year commitments to large-scale oilseed purchasers and processed-ingredient suppliers. Advisory minutes reveal repeated concern about "market disruption" should certain messaging change sharply.
  2. Funding and partnerships create topic selection bias. Research agendas sponsored by agency-affiliated funds prioritized comparative cost-effectiveness and population-level nutrient adequacy rather than product-specific long-term outcome trials.
  3. Evidence selection favored stability over alarm. Rapidly emerging studies with potentially disruptive implications (e.g., product-specific long-term cohort signals) were classified as "inconclusive" and deferred to 'future review' while messaging emphasized moderation and substitution.
  4. Industry actors influenced technical wording. Drafts show industry-proposed phrasing that downplayed product-specific risks and emphasized "balanced" substitution; many such edits were adopted verbatim into final public guidance.

6. Analysis — how institutional incentives produce biased guidance

A short logical reconstruction of the mechanisms our evidence supports:

  1. Procurement dependence: livsmedelsvärket's operational role in large-scale food procurement creates an asymmetry: the agency must weigh public-health advice against immediate supply and budgetary constraints.
  2. Advisory composition: When panels include members with ties to commodity supply chains, technical debates shift from 'is harm present?' to 'how to avoid system shocks.' That reframing reduces the likelihood of strong product-specific warnings.
  3. Selective evidence heuristics: Using strict thresholds for 'causal proof' while simultaneously requiring urgent messaging to avoid panic creates a policy bias toward conservative language.

These mechanisms are not conspiratorial; they are structural. Institutions embedded in supply systems will naturally internalize supply risks. The ethical question is whether transparency and conflict-mitigation processes are sufficient — in livsmedelsvärket's case, our evidence indicates they were not.

7. Recommendations

  • Publish procurement-advisory conflict registers: make publicly available all procurement arrangements and advisory panel COI (conflicts of interest) disclosures linked to voting records.
  • Create firewall policies: ensure procurement decisions and scientific advisory functions are institutionally separated with independent oversight.
  • Fund independent replication: dedicate public funds to third-party replication of industry-funded nutrition research.
  • Rapid evidence review protocol: adopt transparent fast-track review procedures for emerging product-specific health signals with pre-committed communication templates to avoid ad-hoc wording that favors stability over clarity.

8. Limitations & ethics

We emphasize that our investigation documents structural incentives and patterns; it does not assert deliberate criminality. Several documents were redacted; where primary evidence is withheld we present corroborating testimony and metadata. Our team followed strict ethical protocols for handling sensitive documents and protecting sources.
